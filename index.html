Architectural History and the Sexual Liberation Movement

How has architecture both shaped and responded to the evolution of sexual norms? This research examines the spatial dimensions of sexual liberation, analyzing how architecture and urban planning reflect shifting attitudes toward sex, privacy, gender roles, and bodily autonomy.

Research Abstract / Arc of Argument

From the private domestic sphere of the Victorian era, to the sexual openness of countercultural movements in the 1960s and 70s, to contemporary debates around sex-positive urbanism, architecture has played a crucial role in either restricting or enabling sexual expression. Using Michel Foucault’s biopolitics and Henri Lefebvre’s theory of social space, this study explores how built environments regulate sexuality—whether through moralistic zoning laws, feminist reclaiming of space, the rise of erotic leisure architecture, or contemporary policies on public intimacy.

Michel Foucault: The History of Sexuality

"The notion that repression is the fundamental link between power, knowledge, and sexuality appears to me wholly inadequate. Rather than a massive censorship, beginning with the verbal proprieties imposed by the Age of Reason, what was involved was the regulated and polymorphous incitement to discourse."(p. 12)

This passage criticises the traditional repressive hypothesis, arguing that rather than exerting power by repressing sexuality, modern society is constantly creating discussions about sexuality that prompt people to talk and think about it in new ways.

"Sexuality must not be thought of as a stubborn drive by which we attempt to resist the law, but rather as a complex political network. It is the name that can be given to a historical construct: not a furtive reality that is difficult to grasp, but a great surface network in which the stimulation of bodies, the intensification of pleasures, the incitement to discourse, the formation of special knowledges, the strengthening of controls and resistances, are linked to one another, in accordance with a few major strategies of knowledge and power.(p. 105-106)"

Foucault argues that sexuality is not a hidden, essential reality, but rather a historical construct that is incorporated into a whole network of power-knowledge.

"For millennia, man remained what he was for Aristotle: a living animal with the additional capacity for a political existence; modern man is an animal whose politics places his existence as a living being in question.(p. 143)"

Here Foucault introduces the concept of biopower, the idea that modern power goes beyond legal constraints and reaches into the management of life itself, including the control of population, health, sexuality and reproduction.

Henri Lefebvre: The Production of Space

"(Social) space is a (social) product. … The space thus produced also serves as a tool of thought and of action; that in addition to being a means of production it is also a means of control, and hence of domination, of power."(p. 26)

Lefebvre's central argument is that space does not exist neutrally or naturally, but is the result of social production. Space is not only a passive context, but also becomes an instrument of power control and social relations.

Public space is produced via the interaction between different elements

‘Lefebvre’s conceptual model of the production of space’, ResearchGate. Available at: https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Lefebvres-Conceptual-Model-of-the-Production-of-Space_fig3_337570007 (Accessed: 1 March 2025).

“(1)Spatial practice, which embraces production and reproduction, and the particular locations and spatial sets characteristic of each social formation; (2) representations of space, which are tied to the relations of production and to the ‘order’ which those relations impose; (3) representational spaces, embodying complex symbolisms, sometimes coded, sometimes not, linked to the clandestine or underground side of social life, as also to art.”(p. 33)

Lefebvre proposed a threefold concept of space: Spatial practice - the use of space in everyday life, with an emphasis on physicality and perceptibility. Representations of space - the rational conception of space by planners, governments, and scientific and technological fields. Representational space - art, symbols and people's experience of space, subjective and creative.

“The capitalist mode of production tends to create an abstract space which, in attempting to master nature and achieve homogeneity, reduces differences and particularities.”(p. 49)

Capitalism produces an abstract space through standardisation, commodification and rational planning, which tends to homogenise and eliminate local identities in order to allow for more efficient capital circulation and control.

“The struggle for space is not merely a struggle over land or property, but a struggle over meaning, representation, and lived experience.”(p. 52)

According to Lefebvre, space is not only an object of struggle for physical resources, but also a struggle about meaning, representation and experience. He proposes the concept of differential space, which refers to alternative spaces that counter the dominant capitalist space, such as community resistance, autonomous spaces and creative reuse of urban space.

Molgun, E. (2019) ‘Reflections on privacy and publicity in the age of social media by Beatriz Colomina’, *Ece Molgun Blog*. Available at: https://ecemolgun.wordpress.com/2019/03/24/reflections-on-privacy-and-publicity-in-the-age-of-social-media-by-beatriz-colomina/ (Accessed: 1 March 2025).

Colomina, B. (1992). Privacy and Publicity: Modern Architecture as Mass Media. MIT Press.

"Modern architecture only becomes modern with its engagement with the media. It is not simply that the media represents architecture but that architecture is itself a media, a system of representation."(p. 73)

According to Colomina, modern architecture is not defined solely by its physical structure, but shapes and communicates itself through media (photography, publishing, exhibitions). Architecture is not only presented by the media, but architecture itself becomes a medium.

"The house is a mechanism for defining the very relationship between the public and the private, a site where new spatial and visual conditions produce new social relations."(p. 129)

She pointed out that modern architecture, especially residential design, has reshaped the boundaries between the private and the public. Architectural space is not only physical, but also a product of social relations.

Careaga, M. (n.d.) ‘Le Corbusier and the non-representation of technology’, *Medium*. Available at: https://medium.com/@markcareaga/le-corbusier-and-the-non-representation-of-technology-50e35bac62d9 (Accessed: 3 March 2025).

"Le Corbusier’s work is inseparable from its images. His architecture is not simply built, it is published, exhibited, photographed—it exists in a system of representation as much as in the real world."(p. 167)

Le Corbusier is not only an architect, but also a communicator who makes good use of media. His architecture exists not only in real space, but also in media such as publications, photography and exhibitions, which makes his architecture more of a visual cultural phenomenon.

"Transparency in modern architecture does not simply imply openness or accessibility; it is a mechanism of control, surveillance, and the production of new subjectivities."(p. 213)

Colomina critically analyses the modernist promotion of ‘transparency’, pointing out that glass architecture does not only symbolise openness, but also involves surveillance, control and the remodelling of subjectivity.
